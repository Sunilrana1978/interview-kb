Architecture

Walk me through an enterprise integration architecture you designed — what were the key decisions?

+
"I'll walk you through a supply chain integration programme I architected — it's a good example because it forced every major integration trade-off in one engagement."
Situation
The client operated a fragmented supply chain: on-premise SAP ERP, a 3PL warehouse system, a demand planning tool, and a supplier portal — all wired together via brittle point-to-point file transfers and nightly batch jobs. Any upstream delay cascaded into missed fulfilment windows downstream.
Task
Design an integration architecture that decoupled these systems, introduced real-time inventory visibility, and supported an 18-month migration to a cloud-native order management platform — without disrupting live trading.
Action
I started by building a canonical domain model for the Order and Inventory domain — a single agreed schema all systems would produce and consume, breaking the cycle of every system having its own interpretation of what an "order" was. I then chose an event-driven backbone using AWS EventBridge with SNS/SQS for reliable fan-out delivery and dead-letter queues for failure handling. I introduced an API gateway layer for synchronous partner integrations and an event-sourced audit trail for downstream finance and compliance consumers. For migration, I used a strangler-fig approach — routing traffic through the new gateway while legacy batch flows were replaced one domain at a time. Each cutover included a parallel-run period with automated event reconciliation before the legacy route was decommissioned.
Result
Inventory update latency dropped from overnight batch to under 3 minutes. Eliminated 14 point-to-point integrations. The platform handled its first post-migration peak trading period with zero integration failures — compared to 3 major incidents in the prior year's peak.

Event-driven

How have you designed event-driven or async messaging architectures? What pitfalls did you encounter?

+
"Event-driven architecture is where I've done some of my deepest work — including learning the hard lessons around ordering guarantees and schema drift in production systems."
Situation
We were decomposing a monolithic order processing system into microservices. The challenge: ensuring order lifecycle events — created, allocated, picked, packed, shipped, returned — were processed reliably across eight downstream consumers including finance, warehouse, CRM, and customer notifications, each with different latency and consistency requirements.
Task
Design an event backbone that was reliable, observable, and schema-safe — without introducing tight coupling between producers and consumers or a central point of failure.
Action
I implemented a pub/sub pattern using Apache Kafka with topic partitioning by order ID to guarantee per-order message ordering across consumers. Every event carried an idempotency key to handle at-least-once delivery safely — consumers could deduplicate without losing events. We adopted Avro schemas registered in a schema registry with backward-compatibility enforcement — every schema change passed a compatibility gate before deployment, preventing consumer breakage. I added dead-letter topics with automated alerting and a replay mechanism so failed events could be reprocessed without manual intervention. The hardest production issue we hit: consumer lag during a traffic spike caused stale reads in the warehouse system — we solved this with lag-based autoscaling of consumer groups using a custom CloudWatch metric.
Result
Zero duplicate order events processed post-launch across six months. Schema registry prevented two breaking changes that would have caused consumer outages. Consumer autoscaling kept p99 processing latency under 800ms during 4x normal traffic — a peak the old synchronous system could not have handled.

Leadership

Tell me about a time you influenced a technical direction without direct authority.

+
"Most of my architecture influence has happened without direct authority — I've learned that the best technical decisions come from building consensus, not from having the loudest voice in the room."
Situation
Three independent engineering teams were each building their own customer data integration — resulting in three different canonical models for the same Customer entity. Each team had a legitimate reason for their choices based on their system's constraints, but product and analytics were unaware of the divergence. The risk: downstream DTC personalisation and reporting would receive conflicting customer records.
Task
Bring three autonomous teams to a single shared Customer domain model and common integration pattern — without slowing any of their delivery timelines or being perceived as a central bottleneck.
Action
I resisted the urge to propose a solution immediately. Instead, I mapped the three models side by side and documented the conflicts — not solutions, just facts — then shared it with all three tech leads before any meeting. I ran a facilitated design session where the map was the conversation anchor, not my proposal. I asked questions rather than advocating: "What would break for you if the Customer entity included these fields?" I actively listened for the constraints behind each team's choices. A shared model emerged from the conversation itself — I wrote it up, got written sign-off from each team and the product director, and worked with each squad to define a 2-sprint migration path that didn't disrupt their delivery commitments. I then introduced a lightweight API design review process — a 30-minute pre-build conversation — to catch this class of divergence earlier.
Result
Single canonical Customer model adopted across all three teams within 6 weeks. The design review process caught 4 additional divergence risks in the following quarter before they reached production. The product director cited this as a turning point in cross-team technical collaboration.

Modernization

How have you approached modernizing or simplifying a legacy integration platform?

+
"Legacy modernisation is never just a technology problem — it's a risk management programme. I've learned to approach it as a series of controlled, reversible steps rather than a big-bang cutover."
Situation
A retail client was running a 12-year-old on-premise ESB (IBM MQ + custom adapters) connecting ERP, POS, e-commerce, and finance systems. There were 60+ active integration flows, minimal documentation, and two engineers holding all the tribal knowledge. The business had a hard 2-year deadline for cloud migration. Any disruption to order or inventory flows would directly impact trading revenue.
Task
Design a transitional architecture and migration roadmap that would move integrations to a cloud-native platform without disrupting live trading — sequenced by business value and risk, not technical convenience.
Action
I began with a dependency mapping and business criticality assessment — scoring each of the 60 integration flows by transaction volume, revenue impact, failure consequence, and change frequency. This produced a migration wave plan: high-value, lower-risk flows first; complex, deeply coupled flows last. For the technical migration pattern I chose Strangler Fig: introduced an API gateway as the stable routing layer, then incrementally redirected traffic flow by flow as each was rebuilt on the cloud platform. The legacy ESB became a passthrough rather than the hub. For DTC order flows — the highest value, highest risk — I designed a CQRS pattern with event sourcing to support both the legacy read model (needed by existing reporting) and the new microservice write model simultaneously during the transition. Each cutover included a parallel-run period — both old and new routes processed events, and an automated reconciliation service flagged discrepancies. We only decommissioned the legacy route after 5 consecutive clean days.
Result
42 of 60 flows migrated in 14 months with zero trading incidents. Legacy ESB decommissioned on schedule, saving $340K/year in licensing and infrastructure. The new cloud platform handled 3x peak load on the first post-migration Black Friday — a load the old ESB had historically struggled with.

Business alignment

How do you translate business requirements into integration architecture decisions?

+
"I treat integration architecture as a business capability problem first and a technical problem second — the pattern choice should follow from the business constraints, not lead them."
Situation
A product creation team needed to integrate PLM (Product Lifecycle Management) with the supplier collaboration portal. The stated requirement was: "suppliers should see approved specs the same day they're finalised." Taken literally, this sounded like a real-time synchronous integration — complex, expensive, and fragile given the scale of supplier volume involved.
Task
Understand the actual latency and reliability requirements behind the business language, surface hidden constraints, and propose a pragmatic integration architecture that solved the real problem — not the stated one.
Action
I ran a structured requirements discovery session with both business stakeholders and technical leads — asking the questions that aren't in the brief: "What happens to the supplier if a spec arrives 2 hours late? What if it arrives twice? What's the business cost of a spec error reaching a supplier before review is complete?" The answers were revealing: same-day delivery was acceptable, not real-time — but correctness was critical, because a bad spec reaching a supplier could cause production errors worth tens of thousands. This completely changed the architecture decision. Rather than a synchronous REST integration (fast but fragile and potentially delivering incomplete specs), I designed an event-driven async pattern with a review-and-publish workflow. Specs were published as immutable, versioned events only after a formal approval step — consumers received a reliable, auditable feed with full provenance. No partial updates, no race conditions.
Result
Integration built and deployed 40% faster than the original REST-sync estimate due to reduced complexity. Zero spec correction incidents in the first 6 months — compared to 3–4 per month with the prior manual email-based process. Business stakeholders described it as "finally getting the right information at the right time."

Mentorship

How have you upskilled or mentored engineering teams on integration and data architecture patterns?

+
"My goal as a principal architect is to make myself unnecessary — by building the team's capability to make good integration decisions without needing me in every room."
Situation
Joining a programme with 5 engineering squads, I found significant variation in integration quality — some teams produced well-structured RESTful APIs with clear contracts, others used chatty synchronous chains, undocumented payload schemas, and point-to-point database reads across service boundaries. The variation was creating hidden coupling and causing integration failures that were only discovered at system test.
Task
Build shared integration capability across all squads without becoming a bottleneck, without slowing delivery, and without creating the impression of an ivory-tower architecture team imposing standards from above.
Action
I took three parallel approaches: First, I established a fortnightly architecture guild — a 45-minute peer-led session where teams brought live integration design decisions for group discussion. No presentations, no readouts; just real problems and real trade-offs with facilitated discussion. I rotated facilitation to develop leadership across the squads. Second, I built a living integration pattern library — short, opinionated, searchable docs covering our approved patterns (async event, API gateway, canonical model, dead-letter handling, outbox) with worked examples, anti-patterns, and decision criteria. Teams could self-serve before starting integration work. Third, I introduced lightweight pre-build design reviews — a 30-minute conversation with the squad's tech lead before integration work began. Enough to catch structural issues when they were cheap to fix, not enough to create a gate. I also paired with junior engineers during delivery on two squads — not to write the code, but to ask the architecture questions out loud while they worked.
Result
Integration-related issues caught pre-build rose by 60% (vs post-merge or post-test). Three senior engineers from the guild began running their own cross-squad design sessions by month 4. The pattern library became the onboarding reference for all new engineers — time to first integration contribution dropped from 3 weeks to 1. I used the phrase "scale the organisation's data and integration literacy" to describe the programme internally — it became how the team talked about what we were doing together.

Legacy → Modern integration patterns

These are the patterns Columbia will expect you to know and have used. Each card includes what it is, when to use it, the tooling, and a ready-to-use sentence that showcases your hands-on experience. Pick 3–4 you've genuinely used and make them your story anchors.

Migration & coexistence strategies
Strangler Fig
Low-risk migration

Incrementally replace a legacy system by routing traffic through a new layer. New capabilities are built alongside the old system — which remains live — until it can be safely decommissioned, domain by domain.

When to use

Cannot afford a big-bang cutover. Business must remain live during migration. Classic for ESB, monolith decomposition, on-premise ERP integrations. The safe default for most enterprise migration programmes.

AWS API GatewayAzure API ManagementKong / NginxFeature flags
Showcase sentence
"I used Strangler Fig to migrate 42 legacy ESB flows to a cloud-native platform over 14 months with zero trading incidents — routing all traffic through an API gateway and cutting over flow by flow, with a parallel-run reconciliation gate before each decommission."
Anti-Corruption Layer (ACL)
Data integrity

A translation boundary that prevents the legacy system's messy data model from infecting the modern domain model. The ACL translates between legacy schemas and your canonical model in both directions — modern systems never see legacy internals.

When to use

Legacy system has inconsistent or poorly modelled data (very common in ERP, WMS, PLM). Essential when you want the modern platform to use a clean canonical model without inheriting years of technical debt.

MuleSoft DataWeaveAWS GlueCustom transformer serviceApache Camel
Showcase sentence
"I designed an ACL between SAP ERP and a new cloud-native OMS — it translated SAP's internal material codes and org-structure fields into our canonical Product and Order domain model, protecting the modern platform from any SAP schema changes."
Parallel Run
Risk mitigation

Both legacy and modern integrations run simultaneously. Outputs are compared via automated event reconciliation — discrepancies are flagged before the legacy route is decommissioned. Provides a reversible, evidence-based cutover gate.

When to use

High-value, high-risk flows where correctness must be proven before full cutover. Critical for financial flows, supply chain allocation, and order state transitions where errors have direct revenue or compliance impact.

Custom reconciliation serviceAWS Step FunctionsData diff toolingKafka dual-publish
Showcase sentence
"For every migrated flow I ran a 2-week parallel period — old and new routes both processed events, and an automated reconciliation service flagged discrepancies. We only decommissioned the legacy route after 5 consecutive clean days with zero divergence."
Change Data Capture (CDC)
Legacy bridge

Stream changes from a legacy database as events without modifying the legacy application. CDC monitors the database transaction log and publishes row-level changes as domain events — zero changes to legacy source code required.

When to use

Legacy system cannot be modified (COTS, ERP, mainframe). Source API doesn't exist or can't support the required throughput. Ideal for WMS, ERP, and legacy mainframe systems where application-layer access is limited.

DebeziumAWS DMSOracle GoldenGateKafka Connect
Showcase sentence
"We couldn't touch the legacy WMS source code, so I used Debezium CDC on the SQL Server database to capture inventory changes as events, published them to Kafka, and consumed them in the new inventory microservice — zero modifications to the legacy system."
Async & event-driven patterns
Event-Driven Architecture
Decoupling

Producers publish domain events to a broker; consumers react independently. No direct coupling between systems. The broker provides durability, ordering guarantees, and fan-out to multiple consumers from a single event publication.

When to use

Multiple consumers need the same event. Producer should not know about consumers. High throughput, loose coupling required. Supply chain events (order placed, shipment dispatched, inventory adjusted) are natural fits for this pattern.

Apache KafkaAWS EventBridgeAzure Service BusAWS SNS/SQS
Showcase sentence
"I designed an EDA backbone for order fulfilment — the OMS published OrderConfirmed events to Kafka, consumed independently by warehouse, finance, and notifications services, eliminating synchronous coupling and making each consumer independently deployable and scalable."
Saga Pattern
Distributed transactions

Manages multi-step business transactions across services using local transactions and compensating actions for failure rollback. Two flavours: choreography (decentralised, event-driven) and orchestration (central coordinator defines the sequence).

When to use

Cannot use distributed ACID transactions across service boundaries. Order checkout (reserve inventory → capture payment → create order), supply chain allocation, and returns processing are classic saga candidates.

AWS Step FunctionsTemporalApache CamelCustom orchestrator
Showcase sentence
"For the DTC checkout flow I implemented an orchestration saga using AWS Step Functions — coordinating inventory reservation, payment capture, and order creation with explicit compensating transactions at each step, giving us reliable consistency without distributed locks."
CQRS + Event Sourcing
Read/write split

CQRS separates the write model (commands) from the read model (queries). Event sourcing persists state as an immutable sequence of events — current state is derived by replaying events. Enables parallel legacy and modern models during migration.

When to use

Auditability or regulatory traceability is required. Read and write loads differ significantly. Need to support legacy read model and new write model simultaneously during a system migration — the classic transitional architecture use case.

EventStoreDBKafka + projectionsAWS DynamoDB StreamsAxon Framework
Showcase sentence
"During ERP migration I used CQRS to run the legacy read model and the new microservice write model in parallel — legacy reporting kept querying the existing read store while new orders wrote through the modern command service, giving us a clean, low-risk cutover path."
Outbox Pattern
Reliable messaging

Guarantees that a database write and a message publication happen atomically. The service writes to an "outbox" table in the same DB transaction; a relay process reads the outbox and publishes to the broker — eliminating the dual-write race condition that causes lost events.

When to use

Must guarantee "write to DB and publish event" are atomic — no lost events under failure or load. Critical for financial events, order state changes, and any flow where a lost event has business impact.

Debezium outbox connectorKafka ConnectCustom relay service
Showcase sentence
"We had a dual-write bug causing lost order events under load peaks. I introduced the outbox pattern — the order service writes to both orders table and an outbox table in one transaction; Debezium CDC relay publishes outbox records to Kafka — lost events dropped to zero permanently."
API & gateway patterns
API Gateway / Facade
Stable entry point

A single, stable entry point for external consumers that abstracts the backend topology. Routes requests transparently to legacy or modern services — enabling incremental backend migration without ever changing the consumer-facing contract.

When to use

External consumers (partners, mobile apps, DTC channels) need stable API contracts while backend systems evolve or migrate. Central to the Strangler Fig pattern and every enterprise modernisation programme.

AWS API GatewayAzure API ManagementKongMuleSoft
Showcase sentence
"I introduced AWS API Gateway as the stable facade for our supplier portal — routing to the legacy ERP adapter initially, then cutting over to the new microservice without any supplier-facing API contract change or partner re-integration effort."
Backend for Frontend (BFF)
DTC / mobile

A dedicated API layer tailored to each frontend consumer type (mobile app, web, partner portal). Each BFF aggregates and transforms data from multiple backend services, reducing over-fetching, N+1 calls, and frontend-backend coupling.

When to use

DTC channels with different data needs — mobile needs lightweight, fast payloads; web needs richer data; wholesale portal needs bulk query support. Protects backend services from being shaped by frontend concerns.

Node.js / ExpressGraphQL / AppSyncAzure API ManagementAWS Lambda
Showcase sentence
"For a DTC mobile relaunch I designed a BFF in Node.js that aggregated product, inventory, and pricing from three backend services into a single optimised mobile payload — reducing API calls from 6 per screen to 1 and cutting perceived load time by 40%."
Data integration & canonical model patterns
Canonical Data Model
Data contract

A single agreed-upon schema for a domain entity (Order, Product, Customer, Shipment) that all systems produce and consume. Each system adapts to the canonical model at its own boundary — preventing schema proliferation across a multi-system landscape.

When to use

Multiple systems represent the same domain entity with different schemas. Essential in multi-brand, multi-system environments — exactly Columbia's situation with 4 brands and systems spanning ERP, PLM, WMS, OMS, and DTC.

Confluent Schema RegistryOpenAPI / AsyncAPIJSON SchemaAvro / Protobuf
Showcase sentence
"I led the design of a canonical Order domain model covering ERP, OMS, and DTC — published as an OpenAPI spec, enforced via schema registry. Three teams with divergent order schemas converged on it within 6 weeks, eliminating an entire class of downstream data conflict."
Data Mesh / Domain Ownership
Scale pattern

Each business domain owns and publishes its data as a product with a defined contract. Consumers subscribe to domain data products rather than calling source systems directly — eliminating central data team bottlenecks and distributing data ownership appropriately.

When to use

Large organisations with multiple autonomous business domains generating and consuming shared data. Applicable to Columbia's multi-brand, multi-domain environment where Supply Chain, Planning, and DTC all produce and consume overlapping data.

AWS Lake FormationAzure PurviewDatabricksdbtDataHub
Showcase sentence
"I introduced data mesh principles to distribute ownership of supply chain data — the inventory team published a certified inventory data product consumed by planning, DTC, and finance, eliminating 5 redundant ETL pipelines and reducing data latency from daily batch to near-real-time."

Questions to ask the interviewer

Strong questions signal strategic thinking and genuine interest. These are ordered from most to least impactful — aim to ask 3–4 across a typical interview slot.

Integration landscape
"What does the current integration platform look like — iPaaS, custom middleware, or a mix? And what's the primary driver pushing you to evolve it now?"
Shows you're thinking about the real problem they need solved, not just the title. Positions you as someone who assesses before prescribing.
Modernisation maturity
"Where are you in the legacy-to-modern journey — have canonical domain models been established for the key domains, or is that still early work? Which domain feels most urgent?"
Signals architecture maturity and lets you connect your experience with canonical models and migration patterns directly to their situation.
Scope & governance
"How does the Enterprise Integration Architecture team work with brand-level engineering teams for Mountain Hardwear, Sorel, and prAna — federated model or centralised standards with local delivery?"
Shows you understand multi-brand complexity and the governance challenge. Signals you're thinking about how to be effective, not just about the role description.
Domain priorities
"Which domain — Supply Chain, DTC, or Product Creation — has the most pressing integration challenges right now, and what's the root cause of that urgency?"
Lets you reference relevant patterns from your experience naturally. Opens a conversation where you can demonstrate strategic thinking rather than just answering questions.
First 90 days
"What does success look like in the first 90 days — is it more about landscape understanding and relationship building, delivering against a specific initiative, or establishing architectural standards?"
Demonstrates you're already thinking about delivery and alignment, not just the offer. Shows executive maturity and orientation to outcomes.
AI & innovation
"The role mentions embracing AI to drive innovation — are there specific integration or data movement problems where you're already exploring GenAI or ML-assisted automation?"
Ties directly to their stated requirement and opens a forward-looking conversation where you can share your own perspective on AI in integration workflows.
