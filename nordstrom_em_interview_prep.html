L-01 How do you manage a team that includes both full-time engineers and contractors? How do you keep them aligned? +
LeadershipBehavioral
Strategy
Treat this as a question about inclusion and clarity, not about managing two tiers. Key principle: contractors need crystal-clear scope and success criteria; FTEs need growth, ownership, and context.
Answer Points
  • Start with a unified team charter: shared definition of done, on-call rotation expectations, and communication norms regardless of employment type.
  • Give contractors well-scoped, clearly bounded work with explicit handoff points — reduces ambiguity and context-switching overhead.
  • Reserve platform ownership, architecture decisions, and ambiguous problem spaces for FTEs to build institutional knowledge.
  • Run inclusive ceremonies: all-hands sprint reviews, shared retrospectives — avoid contractor "other-ing."
  • Watch for knowledge concentration risk: document everything contractors touch before contracts end.
Your Nike Hook
At Nike, describe a specific project where you worked across FTEs and vendor/contract engineers. Quantify scale or outcome if possible.
L-02 Tell me about a time you gave direct, difficult feedback to an engineer. What was the outcome? +
LeadershipBehavioral
Strategy
This tests psychological safety + managerial courage. Interviewers want to hear that you gave the feedback directly (not softened to uselessness), that you owned the discomfort, and that you cared about the outcome for the person — not just the performance metric.
STAR Frame
  • Situation: Name a real scenario — delivery risk, interpersonal friction, technical stubbornness, or missed growth opportunity.
  • Task: Your responsibility as the manager to address it promptly, not let it fester.
  • Action: Scheduled 1:1, came with specific observable examples (not generalizations), named the impact on the team or product, invited their perspective, then co-built a plan.
  • Result: What changed — behavior, trajectory, relationship. If they left the team, own that too and explain what you'd do the same or differently.
Your Nike Hook

L-03 How do you balance keeping engineers in flow (deep work) with your need for visibility and reporting up? +
Leadership
Strategy
This is about async-first culture and trust. Nordstrom's stated value is "with agility" — they don't want a micromanager.
  • Establish lightweight status norms: async weekly written update over status meetings. Engineers write it; you synthesize upward.
  • Reserve synchronous time for decisions, blockers, and design — not for reporting.
  • Use OKR/milestone tracking dashboards (JIRA, Linear, etc.) so visibility is ambient, not extracted.
  • For stakeholder reporting: build a predictable cadence (weekly email, monthly review) so engineers know what's expected and aren't interrupted ad hoc.
  • Protect deep work blocks on the team calendar — no meetings 9–12 is a policy worth defending.
L-04 Describe how you've developed engineers on your team. What's your framework for growth conversations? +
LeadershipBehavioral
Strategy
Demonstrate intentionality — growth doesn't happen by accident. Show you have a system.
  • Start with a "career map" conversation in the first 30 days: where do they want to be in 2 years? What skills do they need? What kind of problems energize them?

  • Sponsor, not just mentor: actively put their names in rooms they're not in.
L-05 How do you handle an engineer who is technically strong but creates friction on the team? +
LeadershipBehavioral
Strategy
Classic "brilliant jerk" question. Show you don't let technical skill excuse behavioral impact — but that you approach it with curiosity first, not punishment.
  • First, diagnose: is this friction from high standards, miscommunication style, or genuine disrespect? The root cause changes the response.
  • Name the impact specifically: "When you cut off X in retro, here's what happened to team psychological safety."
  • Give them ownership: "I need you to be a force multiplier on this team. Your technical judgment is strong — how do you want to channel that into making others better?"
  • Set a clear behavior bar with a timeline. Revisit in 30/60 days.
  • If pattern persists: a technically strong but team-damaging engineer is net-negative at scale. Be willing to act.

T-01 Design a product catalog event pipeline that propagates price and availability changes across Nordstrom's systems in near real-time. +
TechnicalDomain
Design Approach
  • Source: CDC (Change Data Capture) on the product master database — Debezium or equivalent — publishing change events to Kafka topics partitioned by product_id.
  • Schema governance: Confluent Schema Registry with Avro schemas. Enforce backward compatibility — downstream consumers never break on new optional fields.
  • Processing: Stream processors (Kafka Streams / Apache Flink) enrich raw events with product hierarchy (brand, category, typecode) from the reference data layer.
  • Sinks: BigQuery gold layer tables for analytics; downstream REST/event APIs for personalization and supply chain; search index (Elasticsearch/Solr) for product search freshness.
  • Data quality gates: Dead-letter queues for malformed events; reconciliation jobs comparing warehouse counts to source truth nightly.
  • Observability: Event lag metrics per topic, schema evolution alerts, consumer group lag dashboards in Datadog/Grafana.
Your Nike Hook
Map this to Nike's PLM pipeline: "At Nike, when a style attribute changed in the product lifecycle system, we propagated that event downstream to…" — use the actual topology you know.
T-02 How would you approach migrating the team's existing data pipelines to a medallion architecture without breaking downstream consumers? +
Technical
Migration Principles
  • Strangler Fig pattern: Build new medallion layers in parallel. Don't cut over — run dual-write until downstream consumers are migrated and validated.
  • Layer by layer: Bronze first (raw ingest, immutable, with partitioning and schema enforcement). Then Silver (clean, deduplicated, type-cast). Then Gold (business-domain views for ML, reporting).
  • Contract-first with downstream: Before deprecating any legacy table, get explicit sign-off from every consumer team — ML, personalization, supply chain — with a sunset date they agreed to.
  • Data quality parity gates: Don't declare Bronze→Gold complete until row counts, null rates, and key metrics match legacy output within an agreed tolerance.
  • Terraform everything: New layer infrastructure (GCS/S3 buckets, BigQuery datasets, Kafka topics) is code from day one. No console clicking.
  • Team sequencing: Run a pilot on one domain (e.g., vendor data) before scaling to the full product catalog. Capture learnings in an ADR.
T-03 How do you ensure data quality and freshness in a gold-layer table consumed by both ML models and human-facing dashboards? +
Technical
Answer Points
  • Define SLOs per consumer type: ML batch training may tolerate T-1 day; dashboard refresh needs <15 min lag; search index needs <5 min. Design pipeline SLAs around the strictest requirement.
  • Automated data quality checks: Great Expectations or dbt tests on every pipeline run — null rates, referential integrity, row count thresholds, value range checks.
  • Freshness metadata: Stamp every gold table row with pipeline_run_ts and source_event_ts. Dashboards alert when the gap exceeds the SLO.
  • Quarantine pattern: Failed quality checks route bad rows to a quarantine table with error codes — never silently drop or pass bad data downstream.
  • Lineage tracking: Use a tool like OpenLineage or dbt docs to surface table-level lineage so ML teams know exactly what transformations their training data went through.
T-04 How do you handle schema evolution in a Kafka-based pipeline where multiple teams own different consumer microservices? +
Technical
Answer Points
  • Schema Registry as the contract layer: All producers and consumers register schemas. No schema change without a registry check.
  • Compatibility policy: Enforce BACKWARD_TRANSITIVE compatibility by default — new fields must have defaults; no field deletions or type changes.
  • Change communication process: Producer teams file a "schema change request" with the consumer registry — impacted teams get 2-sprint notice minimum.
  • Versioned topics for breaking changes: If a breaking change is unavoidable, produce to a new topic version (e.g., product-events-v2) and run parallel consumption until all consumers migrate.
  • Consumer resilience: Consumers should be tolerant readers — log and skip unknown fields rather than crashing. This is a coding standard enforced in code review.
T-05 Walk me through how you'd design observability for a data pipeline portfolio — what do you instrument, alert on, and review? +
Technical
Three-Layer Observability
  • Infrastructure layer: Kubernetes pod health, memory/CPU for consumers, Kafka broker metrics (under-replicated partitions, ISR shrinkage), cloud resource quotas.
  • Pipeline layer: Consumer group lag per topic (most critical), throughput (events/sec), error rate, DLQ depth, end-to-end latency (event_time → gold_table_write_time).
  • Data layer: Table freshness (last pipeline_run_ts), row count deltas vs. prior run, null rates on critical fields, SLO breach alerts.
  • Alerting philosophy: Alert on symptoms (SLO breach, consumer lag > N), not causes. PagerDuty for P1 (data freshness SLA missed), Slack for P2 (lag trending up).
  • Weekly review: Error budget burn rate, top DLQ producers, schema drift incidents. Blameless postmortems for any P1 within 48 hours.
T-06 As an EM with technical depth expected — how do you stay close enough to the code without becoming a bottleneck in design decisions? +
TechnicalLeadership
Strategy
  • Participate in architecture reviews and ADR sign-offs — don't write the ADR, but shape the decision criteria and make the call when teams are stuck.
  • Join PR reviews on high-risk or cross-team work — not to nitpick style, but to catch systemic issues (data model choices, API contracts, failure modes).
  • Maintain a "technical reading" habit: schema changes, JIRA epics, runbook updates. This gives ambient awareness without interrupting engineers.
  • Be explicit with the team: "I'm not your code reviewer; I'm your thought partner on design and your shield from stakeholder noise."
  • Set a rule for yourself: never merge code on behalf of an engineer — always coach them through it. Preserve their ownership.

B-02 Describe a time you had to make a significant technical decision with incomplete information and time pressure. +
BehavioralTechnical
Strategy
They want to see structured judgment under uncertainty — not paralysis, not recklessness.
  • Frame how you identified the decision's key unknowns and the cost of being wrong in each direction.
  • Describe what information you gathered fast (time-boxed spike, quick stakeholder consult, precedent research).

  • Explain how you documented the decision (ADR, Slack thread, JIRA comment) and how you communicated risk to stakeholders.

B-03 Tell me about a time your team missed a deadline or had a production incident. How did you handle it? +
Behavioral
Strategy
This tests ownership, blamelessness, and systemic thinking. The worst answer is externalizing blame. The best answer takes ownership, protects the team, and shows systems-level fix.

B-04 How do you prioritize when you have more work than team capacity — and stakeholders all think their project is #1? +
BehavioralStakeholder
Answer Points
  • Make capacity visible: Show stakeholders a capacity model — here's what the team can ship in Q3; here are the 7 asks; here's what "yes to A" means for B, C, D.
  • Prioritize by strategic impact, not loudness: Use a shared scoring rubric (business impact × urgency × effort) reviewed with your director. Removes subjectivity.
  • Escalate trade-off decisions up: "I can do both if we push X to Q4 — that's a business decision, not an engineering one. Let me set up the right conversation."
  • Protect the team from context-switching tax: Half-commits to three projects deliver less than full commitment to one. Be explicit about this cost.
  • Communicate dates, not maybes: Give stakeholders honest timelines with dependencies stated, not optimistic guesses.

S-01 How do you translate complex data infrastructure requirements into something a merchandising business partner can align to? +
Stakeholder
Answer Points
  • Start with the business outcome they care about — "you want pricing changes live in the search results in under 10 minutes" — and work backward to the technical requirements, not forward from architecture diagrams.
  • Use data SLOs as the translation layer: freshness, accuracy, availability — expressed as business terms, not technical metrics.
  • Show impact in their currency: missed pricing update = X% revenue impact; stale catalog data = X% increase in returns.
  • Avoid jargon substitution: don't say "medallion architecture" — say "three-layer data pipeline where raw data gets cleaned and then shaped for your specific reports."
  • Build a shared "data product" contract: business owner agrees on what "good" looks like; team delivers to that spec; deviations are communicated, not silently absorbed.
Your Nike Hook
Reference a specific time you aligned a non-technical Nike stakeholder — supply chain, merchandising, or finance — to a data platform decision.
S-02 Describe a time you had to push back on a stakeholder request that wasn't technically feasible on their timeline. +
StakeholderBehavioral
Strategy
Show confidence with collaboration — you said no to the request, not to the person or the goal.
  • Lead with acknowledgment of the business need: "I understand why Q4 readiness for this data feed matters to the vendor partner program."
  • Be specific about what's not feasible and why — not a vague "it's complicated" but "to do this safely we need X weeks for testing; skipping that creates Y risk."

S-03 How would you manage the brand partner data program — onboarding new external partners while maintaining quality for existing ones? +
StakeholderTechnicalDomain
Answer Points
  • Templatize the onboarding: Build a partner onboarding playbook — standard data feed specs, schema templates, quality thresholds, SLA agreements — so each new partner doesn't require custom engineering.
  • Self-service where possible: Partner-facing data spec documentation, schema validation tools, and sandbox environments reduce engineering touch per onboarding.
  • Isolate partner pipelines: Each partner's data feed should be independently observable and independently degradable — one partner's bad data shouldn't pollute others.
  • Capacity guard: Onboarding N new partners per quarter is a capacity model, not an unlimited commitment. Work with the director to set a sustainable intake rate.
  • Quality gates at ingestion: Validation checks before data enters the silver layer — reject and notify, don't ingest silently corrupted data.
S-04 How do you build trust with ML teams who depend on your data products for model training? +
Stakeholder
Answer Points
  • Establish a data product SLA that ML agrees to — freshness, completeness, schema stability — and measure against it publicly.
  • Give ML teams early warning of any schema change or pipeline migration: they need lead time to retrain or validate models.
  • Create a shared data quality dashboard they can self-serve — don't make ML teams file tickets to find out if data is fresh.
  • Run a quarterly joint review: what did we ship, what broke, what's coming. Treats ML as a partner, not a consumer.
  • Embed a data engineer in ML sprint planning periodically — reduces surprise requests and builds mutual understanding of constraints.
